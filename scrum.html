What is Scrum?

Scrum is a lightweight Agile framework designed for developing, delivering, and maintaining complex products. It emphasizes small, iterative cycles called Sprints, which typically last 2‚Äì4 weeks. During each Sprint, a cross-functional team works on building a "potentially shippable" product increment.

The essence of Scrum lies in its inspect-and-adapt approach:

  • Teams continuously inspect their progress during daily stand-ups
  • They adapt strategies based on feedback from stakeholders
  • They improve processes during retrospectives

Scrum in Real Life

üçï Food Delivery App Example

Imagine you are developing a mobile food delivery app. Instead of building the entire app at once (which might take months), Scrum allows your team to:

Sprint 1 Deliver the login and signup feature
Sprint 2 Add restaurant browsing
Sprint 3 Implement payment integration

This approach ensures that stakeholders see progress regularly and can give feedback early, reducing the risk of failure.

Roles in Scrum

Scrum defines three key roles, each essential for the success of the framework:

üëë Product Owner

  • Represents the voice of the customer
  • Owns the Product Backlog (a list of tasks, features, or bugs)
  • Prioritizes work to maximize the product's value
Example: In a food delivery app, the Product Owner decides whether real-time tracking is more important than adding a review system.

üéØ Scrum Master

  • Acts as a coach and facilitator for the team
  • Ensures the team follows Scrum principles
  • Removes roadblocks (called impediments)
Example: If developers are stuck due to a missing API, the Scrum Master coordinates with other teams to get it resolved.

üë®‚Äçüíª Development Team

  • A self-organizing, cross-functional group
  • Focuses on delivering a "Done," working increment
  • Usually ranges from 3 to 9 members
Note: Includes developers, testers, designers, etc.

Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

Scrum introduces time-boxed events to maintain rhythm and ensure continuous communication.

üèÉ‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Sprint

A fixed duration (usually 2‚Äì4 weeks) where the team works on a set of tasks. The goal is to deliver a potentially shippable product increment.

üìã Sprint Planning

A meeting at the start of the Sprint where the team decides what will be built. The team selects items from the Product Backlog.

Example: "Implement restaurant search feature" for the next 2 weeks.

‚òÄÔ∏è Daily Stand-up

A 15-minute meeting held daily. Each member answers:

  • What did I accomplish yesterday?
  • What will I work on today?
  • Are there any blockers?

üé≠ Sprint Review

Held at the end of the Sprint to showcase completed work to stakeholders. Feedback is collected to improve the product.

üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

Focuses on improving the team's process. Questions discussed:

  • What went well?
  • What didn't go well?
  • What can we improve?

Scrum Artifacts

Artifacts in Scrum are designed to increase transparency and ensure everyone understands the work being done.

üìù Product Backlog

A master list of everything needed in the product, maintained by the Product Owner.

Example: Login system, cart functionality, restaurant ratings, etc.

üìã Sprint Backlog

A subset of the product backlog selected for the current Sprint.

Example: "Add payment gateway" and "Create order history page"

üöÄ Increment

The final, working product delivered after a Sprint. Must meet the Definition of Done.

Must be tested, reviewed, and deployable
